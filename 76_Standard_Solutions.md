# The 76 Standard Solutions of TRIZ

> Compiled by G.S. Altshuller and associates (1975–1985). The 76 Standard Solutions are a set of inventive solution patterns based on Substance-Field (Su-Field) analysis. They are typically used after a Su-Field model is built—often within ARIZ—and constraints on the solution have been identified.

---

## Overview

The 76 Standard Solutions are grouped into **5 classes**:

| Class | Description | Standards |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Improving the system with no or little change | 13 |
| 2 | Improving the system by changing the system | 23 |
| 3 | System transitions | 6 |
| 4 | Detection and measurement | 17 |
| 5 | Strategies for simplification and improvement | 17 |
| | **Total** | **76** |

These solutions address Level 3 inventive problems—those that significantly improve existing systems through resolution of inventive contradictions, often by introducing entirely new elements. They represent approximately 18% of patents.

---

## Su-Field Model Notation

- **S1** = Object (substance acted upon)
- **S2** = Tool (substance that acts)
- **F** = Field (energy/interaction between substances)
- **S3** = Additive substance (introduced to solve the problem)
- **E** = Environment
- **ER** = Environmental Resource

Field types include: mechanical, thermal, chemical, acoustic, electrical, magnetic, gravitational, optical, weak nuclear, and strong nuclear.

## Class 1: Improving the System with No or Little Change

*Modifying a system to achieve a desired outcome or eliminate an undesired outcome with minimal changes. Includes solutions for completing an incomplete model (one that lacks S1, S2, and F, or where the field F is inadequate).*

### 1.1 Improving the Performance of an Inadequate System

#### 1.1.1 — Complete an Incomplete Model
If the Su-Field model is incomplete (missing S1, S2, or F), add the missing element(s) to complete the triad.

**Examples:**
- A hammer alone does nothing. A hammer + nail does nothing. The complete system requires hammer (S2), nail (S1), and mechanical energy (F).
- A truck without fuel won't move. The complete system is the truck, fuel, and conversion from chemical energy to mechanical energy.
- In organizations, a single individual (S2) needs others (S1) and persuasive arguments (F) to accomplish goals.

#### 1.1.2 — Internal Additive
The system cannot be changed but a permanent or temporary additive is acceptable. Incorporate an internal additive in either S1 or S2.

**Examples:**
- Adding aerated slag particles reduces concrete density.
- Doping silicon wafers changes electrical properties.
- Adding baking powder enhances the effect of yeast in bread.
- Adding Vitamin C strengthens the immune system.
- In heart-lung machines, heparin is added to prevent blood clotting (it later metabolizes).

#### 1.1.3 — External Additive
Use a permanent or temporary external additive S3 to change either S1 or S2.

**Examples:**
- Snow (S1) + skis (S2) system improved by adding wax (S3) to the skis.
- Replacing a traditional camera with a digital camera to improve photo conversion workflow.
- Teflon tape added to seal threaded joints in air compressor systems.
- Foam packaging (S3) added around cargo to prevent damage from truck motion.

#### 1.1.4 — Environmental Resource as Additive
Use a resource from the environment as the additive, either internally or externally.

**Examples:**
- A channel marker buoy that rocks too much can use sea water as ballast.
- A leather oil seal that leaks because the leather is dry—use the oil already present in the system to expand the leather and improve the seal.

#### 1.1.5 — Modify the Environment
Modify or change the environment of the system to achieve the desired effect.

**Examples:**
- Desktop computers overheat a room, compromising performance. Air condition the office.
- A person with a cold breathes through their mouth, causing a dry throat. Humidify the air in the room.

#### 1.1.6 — Surplus and Removal (Maximal Mode)
Precise control of small amounts is difficult. Apply a surplus and then remove the excess.

**Examples:**
- Coatings applied in surplus and excess removed by centrifugal force.
- Dipping a handle in paint and allowing gravity to remove excess.
- Overfilling concrete in a form, then removing excess to get fine control of height and level.
- Engraved printing plates flooded with ink; squeegee removes excess before paper contact.
- Injection molding: vent holes allow excess material (sprue) to flow out, then it's trimmed.

#### 1.1.7 — Field Linkage (Indirect Application)
If a moderate field is insufficient but a greater field will damage the system, apply the larger field to another element linked to the original.

**Examples:**
- Double boiler: material cannot withstand direct flame, so flame heats water which heats the inner container (never exceeds boiling point).
- Pre-stressed concrete: cable cannot withstand heat for expansion, so rods connected to cables are heated. When rods cool and shrink, cables achieve proper tension.

#### 1.1.8 — Selective Protection (Maximal Mode with Shielding)
A pattern of large/strong and small/weak effects is required. Protect locations requiring smaller effects with a substance S3.

**Examples:**
- Glass ampoules sealed by flame: immerse in water to protect medicine from heat.
- Heat sinks during soldering protect elements from damage.
- Masks during silicon wafer fabrication allow dopants in certain regions while blocking others.
- Masking tape or stencils control paint application areas.
- Place explosives only where needed to demolish a building with precise timing.

### 1.2 Eliminating or Neutralizing Harmful Effects

#### 1.2.1 — Introduce S3 Between S1 and S2
Useful and harmful effects coexist. It is not necessary for S1 and S2 to be in direct contact. Introduce S3 to remove the harmful effect.

**Examples:**
- Sterile gloves (S3) between doctor's hands (S2) and patient (S1) eliminate germs.
- Steel plate (S3) between house jack (S2) and timber (S1) distributes the load.

#### 1.2.2 — Modify S1 or S2 (Including "Nothing")
New substances cannot be added. Remove the harmful effect by modifying S1 or S2. This includes adding "nothing"—voids, hollows, vacuum, air, bubbles, foam—or adding a field that acts like an additional substance.

**Examples:**
- Gust-proof umbrella: vents and flaps in fabric reduce aerodynamic forces.
- Steel balls wearing a hole in a pipe elbow: a magnetic field causes a layer of balls to form at the elbow, protecting the pipe.
- Thermal contraction used instead of lubricant to fit one part inside another (cool the inner part, insert, let it expand).

#### 1.2.3 — Introduce S3 to Absorb Harmful Field
The harmful action is caused by a field. Introduce S3 to absorb the harmful effects.

**Examples:**
- Lead apron protects patient from x-rays outside the imaging area; lead wall protects technicians.
- Heat sink under electronic component dissipates heat that would warp the circuit board.

#### 1.2.4 — Counteracting Field F2
Useful and harmful effects exist where S1 and S2 must remain in contact. Counteract the harmful effect of F1 by introducing F2 to neutralize it or gain an additional useful effect.

**Examples:**
- Water pump noise: use acoustic field 180° out of phase to cancel sound.
- After tendon surgery: brace immobilizes leg (F1mech) but pulsed electrical field (F2elec) prevents muscle atrophy during therapy.

#### 1.2.5 — Magnetic Compensation
A harmful effect exists due to magnetic properties. Remove it by heating above the Curie point or introducing an opposite magnetic field.

**Examples:**
- Magnetic crane: permanent magnet holds load, electromagnet activates only to release (also provides safety during power failure).
- Vehicle compass: small permanent magnets compensate for magnetic interference from the vehicle.
- Magnetic recording media: heating above Curie temperature disorders all domains for refresh.

## Class 2: Improving the System by Changing the System

*These solutions involve more significant modifications to the system's substance-field model, including transitions to more complex models.*

### 2.1 Transition to a Complex Su-Field Model

#### 2.1.1 — Chain Model
Transition to a chain Su-Field model by introducing a new substance S3 that interacts with the existing elements, creating an additional Su-Field triad.

**Example:** If direct action of S2 on S1 through field F is insufficient, introduce S3 as an intermediary that receives the action of S2 and transmits a modified action to S1.

#### 2.1.2 — Double Model
Transition to a double (internal complex) Su-Field model by introducing a second field F2 that acts on the existing substances.

**Example:** Combine mechanical and electrical fields to achieve both structural support and sensing capability in the same system.

### 2.2 Transition to Enhanced Su-Field Models

#### 2.2.1 — Replace Uncontrolled Field with Controllable One
Replace an uncontrolled or poorly controlled field with one that is more easily managed.

**Example:** Replace gravitational feeding (hard to control) with vibratory feeding (easy to control rate and intensity).

#### 2.2.2 — Increase Fragmentation of S2
Increase the degree of fragmentation (dispersal) of the tool substance S2 to increase its effectiveness.

**Progression:** Solid → Powder → Liquid → Gas → Plasma → Field

**Example:** Solid grinding wheel → abrasive powder → chemical polishing → ion beam polishing.

#### 2.2.3 — Apply Capillary and Porous Structures
Transition to porous or capillary structures in S1 or S2, and fill pores with another substance.

**Example:** Porous metal bearings impregnated with lubricant for self-lubrication.

#### 2.2.4 — Increase Dynamization
Increase the degree of dynamics of the system by making rigid elements more flexible or segmented.

**Progression:** One joint → Many joints → Flexible object → Liquid → Gas → Field

**Example:** Rigid robotic arm → multi-jointed arm → flexible arm → hydraulic arm.

#### 2.2.5 — Structuring Fields
Structure a field (make it non-uniform) to match required spatial or temporal patterns of action.

**Example:** Use standing waves to sort particles by size; use focused laser beams for precision cutting.

#### 2.2.6 — Structuring Substances
Structure a substance (make it non-uniform) to match required spatial or temporal patterns.

**Example:** Composite materials with different properties in different regions; functionally graded materials.

### 2.3 Transition to Rhythmic Actions

#### 2.3.1 — Rhythmic Coordination
Coordinate the rhythms (frequencies) of actions in the system.

**Example:** Match the frequency of applied force to the natural frequency of the system for resonance amplification.

#### 2.3.2 — Increase Frequency
Increase the frequency of the applied field.

**Example:** Ultrasonic cleaning vs. manual scrubbing; ultrasonic welding vs. conventional welding.

#### 2.3.3 — Use Resonance
Exploit resonance by matching the frequency of external action to the natural frequency of the object.

**Example:** Breaking kidney stones with ultrasound at the resonant frequency of the stone.

### 2.4 Transition to Ferromagnetic Substance-Field Models (Fe-SFMs)

#### 2.4.1 — Introduce Ferromagnetic Substance
Replace a plain substance with a ferromagnetic substance (or add ferromagnetic particles) controlled by a magnetic field.

**Example:** Add ferromagnetic particles to a lubricant to create magnetorheological fluid controllable by magnetic field.

#### 2.4.2 — Use Ferromagnetic Particles as Intermediary
Introduce ferromagnetic particles between S1 and S2 to enhance controllability via magnetic field.

**Example:** Ferrofluid seals that use magnetic fields to contain ferromagnetic liquid in place.

#### 2.4.3 — Transition to External Fe-SFM
Use external ferromagnetic elements combined with magnetic field.

**Example:** External magnetic particles on a surface controlled by magnets underneath for material transport.

#### 2.4.4 — Use Magnetic Fluid (Ferrofluid)
Use ferromagnetic liquid (ferrofluid) as a substance in the system.

**Example:** Ferrofluid loudspeakers for improved heat dissipation and damping; ferrofluid seals in rotating shafts.

#### 2.4.5 — Complex Fe-SFM Structures
Combine ferromagnetic substances with other structural approaches (capillary, porous, etc.).

**Example:** Porous matrix filled with ferrofluid for adaptive filtering systems.

#### 2.4.6 — Reverse Fe-SFM
If ferromagnetic particles are used in S2, transition to using the environment or S1 as ferromagnetic instead, or reverse the magnetic field direction.

**Example:** Instead of magnetizing the tool, magnetize the workpiece and use magnetic fields to guide the tool.

#### 2.4.7 — Use Physical Effects with Magnetic Fields
Employ physical effects associated with magnetic fields (Curie point, magnetostriction, Hall effect, etc.).

**Example:** Shape memory alloys controlled by magnetic fields; magnetostrictive actuators for precision positioning.

#### 2.4.8 — Dynamic Fe-SFM
Make the Fe-SFM dynamic by varying field strength, frequency, or configuration over time.

**Example:** Oscillating magnetic field for particle mixing; pulsed magnetic fields for sequential separation.

#### 2.4.9 — Structured Fe-SFM
Structure the magnetic field to create non-uniform effects.

**Example:** Gradient magnetic fields for particle classification by size; patterned magnetic fields for selective assembly.

#### 2.4.10 — Use Interaction Between Ferromagnetic Particles
Exploit the interactions between ferromagnetic particles themselves (chaining, clustering).

**Example:** Chains of magnetic particles forming bristle-like structures for adaptive polishing.

#### 2.4.11 — Combination of Fe-SFM with Additional Fields
Combine ferromagnetic Su-Field with additional fields (electric, thermal, etc.) for multi-functional control.

**Example:** Combined magnetic and electric fields for electro-magneto-rheological fluid with superior control.

#### 2.4.12 — Use of Magnetically Controlled Elastic Properties
Use magnetic fields to control elastic/rheological properties of a ferromagnetic composite system.

**Example:** Magnetorheological elastomers whose stiffness is controlled by applied magnetic field strength.

## Class 3: System Transitions

*Transitions to higher-order systems through combination, integration, or evolution of existing systems.*

### 3.1 Transition to Bi-System or Poly-System

#### 3.1.1 — Combine Identical Systems
Create a bi-system or poly-system by combining two or more identical systems.

**Example:** Double-hulled ship for safety; dual-engine aircraft for reliability; twin-blade razor.

#### 3.1.2 — Combine System with Anti-System
Combine a system with its anti-system (opposite system).

**Example:** Combine heating and cooling elements for precise temperature control; combine tension and compression elements for structural stability.

#### 3.1.3 — Combine Dissimilar Systems
Create a poly-system by combining two or more different (but complementary) systems.

**Example:** Swiss Army knife (multiple tools); smartphone (phone + camera + computer + GPS); pencil with eraser.

#### 3.1.4 — Integrate Parts into a Whole (Folding)
Distribute incompatible properties among the system and its parts, folding the system to integrate them.

**Example:** Bicycle chain—each link is rigid, but the whole is flexible. Chain mail armor—individual links are strong, the whole is conformable.

#### 3.1.5 — Enhance Differences in Poly-Systems
Increase the differences between elements in a poly-system.

**Example:** Multi-grade sandpaper sets with increasingly different grits; color printers using distinct CMYK inks.

### 3.2 Transition to Micro-Level

#### 3.2.1 — Transition from Macro to Micro
Replace a system (or part of it) with a substance that achieves the required function at the micro-level.

**Example:** Replace mechanical locks with molecular-level encryption; replace mechanical mixers with molecular diffusion; transition from bulk materials to nanomaterials.

## Class 4: Detection and Measurement

*Solutions for problems of detection, measurement, and monitoring. The general approach is to convert a measurement problem into a changing/improving problem, or to use indirect measurements.*

### 4.1 Indirect Methods

#### 4.1.1 — Replace Direct Measurement with Measurement of a Copy or Image
Instead of measuring the object directly, measure its copy, photograph, shadow, or model.

**Example:** Measure the diameter of a hot pipe by photographing it and measuring the image. Use shadow patterns to detect surface defects.

#### 4.1.2 — Replace Measurement with Detection of Changes
Instead of measuring absolute values, detect changes in parameters.

**Example:** Instead of measuring exact temperature, detect when it exceeds a threshold using a bimetallic strip that visibly bends.

#### 4.1.3 — Use Successive Detection
Replace a single measurement with two successive detections to identify change over time.

**Example:** Compare two photographs taken at different times to detect structural changes in a building.

### 4.2 Building Measurement Su-Field Models

#### 4.2.1 — Build a Measurement SFM (If None Exists)
If the system cannot be measured, introduce a new Su-Field model specifically for measurement/detection.

**Example:** To detect flow in a pipe, introduce a small vane connected to an electrical sensor.

#### 4.2.2 — Internal or External Complex Measuring SFM
If basic measurement SFM is insufficient, transition to a complex measuring SFM by introducing easily detectable additives.

**Example:** To detect leakage in a refrigerator, mix a luminophore powder with the cooling agent. To detect cracks, use penetrating dyes.

#### 4.2.3 — Use Environmental Resources for Measurement
Use elements already present in the environment as the measuring substance.

**Example:** Use natural thermal radiation of an object for infrared detection rather than introducing a heat source.

#### 4.2.4 — Introduce a Detectable Field
Introduce a field whose parameter indicates the state of the parameter to be measured.

**Example:** Pass electrical current through a liquid to detect when boiling starts (air bubbles dramatically reduce resistance).

### 4.3 Enhancing Measurement Systems

#### 4.3.1 — Use Resonance to Enhance Measurement
Amplify the measured signal by using resonant effects.

**Example:** Use resonant frequency shift to detect micro-level changes in mass or stiffness.

#### 4.3.2 — Use Ferromagnetic Tracers
Introduce ferromagnetic particles and detect them magnetically.

**Example:** Add magnetic ink markers to currency for authentication detection.

### 4.4 Measurement with Fe-SFM

#### 4.4.1 — Replace Standard Measurement with Fe-SFM
Replace conventional measurement elements with ferromagnetic elements measured by magnetic field.

**Example:** Use ferromagnetic fluid level indicator: the fluid level changes the magnetic field detected by a sensor.

#### 4.4.2 — Complex Fe-SFM for Measurement
Use complex ferromagnetic Su-Field models for enhanced measurement.

**Example:** Multiple ferromagnetic markers at known positions for 3D deformation measurement.

#### 4.4.3 — Use Physical Effects with Magnetic Measurement
Employ physical effects associated with magnetism for measurement.

**Example:** Hall effect sensors for non-contact current measurement; magnetostrictive position sensors.

### 4.5 Measurement Direction

#### 4.5.1 — Change the Measurement Direction
If measurement in one direction is difficult, measure in a perpendicular or opposite direction.

**Example:** Measure the deflection of a beam under load horizontally when vertical measurement is obstructed.

#### 4.5.2 — Measure Derivatives or Integrals Instead
If direct measurement of a parameter is difficult, measure its rate of change (derivative) or accumulated value (integral).

**Example:** Instead of measuring instantaneous flow, measure total volume over time.

## Class 5: Strategies for Simplification and Improvement

*Application of known physical, chemical, and geometric effects to simplify and improve systems. Guides the introduction of substances and fields under various constraints.*

### 5.1 Introduction of Substances

#### 5.1.1 — Indirect Introduction
If direct introduction of a substance is not allowed, use indirect methods (introduce temporarily, use derivatives, use by-products).

**Example:** Instead of adding permanent additives, use substances that decompose after performing their function (biodegradable scaffolding).

#### 5.1.2 — Introduction of Fields from the Environment
Introduce a field that is available in the environment or can be derived from environmental resources.

**Example:** Use solar energy for heating rather than introducing electrical heaters; use natural wind for ventilation.

#### 5.1.3 — Use of Inflatable/Removable Substances
Introduce substances that can be inflated during use and removed (or collapsed) afterward.

**Example:** Inflatable molds for concrete casting that are deflated and removed after the concrete sets.

#### 5.1.4 — Derive Substance from Super-System or Sub-System
Instead of introducing an entirely new substance, derive one from the super-system or extract one from the sub-system.

**Example:** Use waste heat from an adjacent manufacturing process rather than adding a new energy source.

### 5.2 Introduction of Fields

#### 5.2.1 — Use Existing Fields
Before introducing new fields, use fields that already exist in the system or its environment.

**Example:** Use vibrations naturally present in a machine for mixing rather than adding a separate mixer.

#### 5.2.2 — Use Fields from the Environment
Use fields available in the environment (gravity, ambient electromagnetic fields, temperature gradients).

**Example:** Use gravity for material transport (chutes, slides) rather than motorized conveyors.

#### 5.2.3 — Use Substances that Generate Required Fields
Introduce substances that are sources of the required field.

**Example:** Introduce exothermic chemical reactions as a heat source; use piezoelectric materials to generate electrical fields from mechanical stress.

### 5.3 Phase Transitions

#### 5.3.1 — Dual-Phase State
Use the transition between two phases of a substance (solid-liquid, liquid-gas, etc.) to achieve the required effect.

**Example:** Use ice/water transition for volume change (ice expands ~9%); use boiling for rapid pressure generation.

#### 5.3.2 — Use Properties of Phase Boundaries
Exploit the unique properties that exist at phase transition boundaries.

**Example:** Super-cooling for sudden crystallization on command; surface tension effects at liquid-gas interface for self-assembly.

#### 5.3.3 — Dual-Phase Substance
Use a substance that simultaneously exists in two phases.

**Example:** Ice-water slurry for precision cooling (maintains exact 0°C); foam (gas-liquid dual phase) for lightweight filling.

#### 5.3.4 — Reversible Phase Transitions
Use transitions that can be reversed (often using physical effects like temperature, pressure, or fields).

**Example:** Shape memory alloys that change shape with temperature and return to original form; thermochromic inks that change color reversibly.

### 5.4 Use of Physical Effects and Phenomena

#### 5.4.1 — Self-Controlled Transitions
Use physical phenomena that are self-controlling (feedback built into the physics).

**Example:** Thermal fuses that melt at exact temperature; bimetallic thermostats that self-regulate.

#### 5.4.2 — Piezoelectric and Coupled Effects
Use coupled physical effects where one parameter's change automatically creates another.

**Example:** Piezoelectric sensors/actuators; thermoelectric cooling (Peltier effect); electro-optic modulators.

### 5.5 Experimental Approaches

#### 5.5.1 — Obtain Particles or Additives by Decomposition
Produce required micro-particles or additives through decomposition of the existing elements.

**Example:** Generate gas bubbles by electrolysis of the liquid already in the system; produce abrasive particles by controlled fracture.

#### 5.5.2 — Obtain Particles by Combining Elements
Produce required substances through the combination of elements present in the system.

**Example:** Generate a protective oxide layer by combining atmospheric oxygen with the metal surface; produce foam by combining liquid and air under agitation.

#### 5.5.3 — Use "Nothing" (Voids, Vacuum, Foam)
Instead of a substance, use voids, hollows, vacuum, or foams.

**Example:** Vacuum insulation; aerogel (mostly air) for thermal protection; hollow microspheres for lightweight fillers.

## Enhanced Perspectives: From 76 to 111 Standards

Based on research by Davide Russo and Stefano Duci (University of Bergamo, 2015), the original 76 standard solutions have been reorganized into a problem-oriented classification system of 111 standards. Key improvements include:

### Problem-Oriented Classification

The new system classifies by **problem type** rather than solution type:

| Category | Code | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| Harmful Actions | H | Excessive or harmful actions to be reduced/eliminated |
| Insufficient Actions | I | Insufficient or missing actions to be enhanced/created |
| Measurement & Detection | M | Problems of sensing and monitoring |

### Action Categories for Harmful/Excessive Actions (H)

- **H.1** — Block the harmful action by adding a substance S3 between tool and object
- **H.2** — Deflect the harmful action by adding a new field F2 acting on F1
- **H.3** — Counteract by modifying the substance (S') or modifying the field
- **H.4** — Make the object insensitive or less sensitive to the harmful action
- **H.5** — Combine the system with another system; develop links between elements
- **H.6** — Reduce the harmful action until no longer harmful; enhance any useful action that was lost

### Action Categories for Insufficient/Missing Actions (I)

- **I.1** — Concentrate the insufficient action
- **I.2** — Enhance with an additive substance
- **I.3** — Enhance with an additional field
- **I.4** — Make the substance more dynamic (flexible, segmented, changing)
- **I.5** — Make the field more dynamic (pulsed, variable, structured)
- **I.6** — Restructure the system to improve action delivery

### Suggestions for Solutions

For adding substances:
- **(S+)** — Add a substance S3 between the tool and the object
- Find substances in the tool, object, environment, or by decomposition
- Consider temporary vs. permanent additions
- Consider voids, foam, bubbles as "substances of nothing"

For adding fields:
- **(F+)** — Add a new field F2 acting on the field F1
- Consider fields with high controllability (magnetic, electric)
- Use resonance and frequency matching
- Structure the field for non-uniform effects

For making substances dynamic:
- Start by dividing into two joint-coupled parts
- Progression: One joint → Many joints → Flexible object → Liquid → Gas → Field

For making fields dynamic:
- Start with constant field, then pulsate
- Match to natural frequencies
- Use field gradients and structured patterns

## Practical Application Workflow

1. **Model the problem** using Su-Field analysis
2. **Identify the problem type**: incomplete model, harmful effect, insufficient effect, measurement problem, or need for system evolution
3. **Select the appropriate class** (1–5)
4. **Identify constraints**: Can new substances be added? Can the system be modified? Are there environmental resources available?
5. **Apply the matching standard** to generate solution concepts
6. **Consider the enhanced 111 standards** for additional solution paths based on the specific action categories (H, I, M)

## Key Principles from Supporting TRIZ Literature

### From the Contradiction Matrix and 40 Inventive Principles

The 76 Standard Solutions complement the 40 Inventive Principles. The mapping between them (per Terninko, Domb, Miller) shows:

- **Segmentation** (Principle 1) ↔ Standard 5.1.2, 2.2.2, 2.2.4, 3.2.1
- **Merging** (Principle 5) ↔ Standards 1.1.2–1.1.5, 3.1.4
- **Dynamism** (Principle 15) ↔ Standard 2.2.4
- **Phase Transition** (Principle 36) ↔ Standards 5.3.1–5.3.4
- **Ferromagnetic** approaches ↔ Standards 2.4.1–2.4.12

### Laws of Technical System Evolution

The standards align with TRIZ evolution patterns:
- **Increasing ideality** — System evolves toward performing function without the system itself (Classes 1, 5)
- **Uneven development** — Components evolve at different rates, creating contradictions (Class 2)
- **Transition to super-system** — Individual systems merge into poly-systems (Class 3)
- **Transition to micro-level** — Systems evolve from macro to micro mechanisms (Standard 3.2.1)
- **Increasing dynamism** — Systems become more flexible and adaptive (Standard 2.2.4)

### Su-Field Analysis as Foundation

The standard solutions are most powerful when combined with proper Su-Field modeling:
1. Identify all substances and fields in the system
2. Map useful and harmful interactions
3. Identify incomplete, complete, or complex models
4. Navigate to the appropriate standard solution class
